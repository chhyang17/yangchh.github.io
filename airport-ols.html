00
Context

Airports in Canada must maintain clear obstacle limitation surfaces (OLS) — defined 3D airspace zones around runways where no object may penetrate without triggering a safety review. These surfaces are defined by ICAO Annex 14 and enforced under Transport Canada regulations.

A regional airport in northern BC required a full spatial assessment of its airspace environs. Dense conifer forest adjacent to the runway approach and transitional zones had never been formally evaluated for OLS compliance — and the airport operator wanted to understand not just which trees posed a hazard, but whether hazard removal could be partially offset by timber recovery revenue.

01
Problem

Three connected problems needed to be solved in sequence — each one feeding the next:

Problem chain (1) No 3D spatial model of OLS surfaces existed — compliance was untested.
(2) Without spatially explicit hazard tree locations and heights, there was no way to prioritize or scope vegetation management.
(3) Without volume estimates, the operator couldn't assess whether timber salvage was financially meaningful.

Each step required a different spatial method — 3D surface modeling, LiDAR point cloud analysis, and individual tree detection — all applied to the same study area and needing to produce outputs that could speak to both safety regulators and forestry planners.

02
Approach
Phase A — OLS Surface Modeling

OLS geometry was constructed in ArcGIS Pro by translating ICAO Annex 14 surface parameters — approach, transitional, inner horizontal, conical, and outer horizontal — into 3D multipatch features anchored to the runway centreline and threshold elevations. A LiDAR-derived DSM was used as the elevation reference for all penetration checks.

Phase B — Hazard Tree Detection

The LiDAR point cloud was processed to generate a Canopy Height Model (CHM) at 0.5m resolution. Individual trees were detected using a local maximum filter on the CHM, with crown delineation performed via watershed segmentation. Each detected tree was attributed with its apex height, crown area, and OLS exceedance value — classifying it as a confirmed hazard, marginal, or clear.

Phase C — Timber Volume Estimation

For trees classified as confirmed hazards, individual tree volume was estimated using allometric equations applied to LiDAR-derived height and crown diameter measurements. Species composition was inferred from point cloud return density and crown morphology. Total recoverable volume was aggregated by stand zone and reported alongside the hazard inventory to support the operator's vegetation management and cost-recovery planning.

The project delivered three linked outputs: a 3D OLS compliance model identifying all penetrating features, a hazard tree inventory with spatial locations and exceedance values, and a timber volume report aggregated by zone to support salvage contracting decisions.

This workflow demonstrated how a single LiDAR dataset can be leveraged across multiple analytical domains — safety compliance, ecological inventory, and resource economics — without additional data acquisition.

Value of integration By connecting OLS analysis directly to timber volume estimation, the operator could present vegetation management as a partially self-funding activity rather than a pure cost — a meaningfully different framing for budget approvals.
