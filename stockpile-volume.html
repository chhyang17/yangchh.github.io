00
Context

Quarry operations require regular stockpile inventory assessments — accurate volume estimates for aggregate piles are needed for production reporting, royalty calculations, and operational planning. Traditional survey methods are time-consuming and difficult to scale across multiple stockpile areas simultaneously.

This project developed a repeatable GIS-based workflow to compute stockpile volumes from two types of high-resolution elevation data: aerial LiDAR for large-area coverage and drone photogrammetry (SfM) for targeted, high-frequency monitoring of individual piles.

01
Problem

Stockpile volumes were previously estimated using manual survey stakes and tape measurements — a method that was slow, weather-dependent, and produced results that varied significantly depending on who conducted the survey and which measurement points they chose.

Accuracy and auditability gap For royalty and compliance reporting, volume estimates need to be defensible and methodologically consistent across time periods. Manual tape surveys couldn't provide either — results weren't reproducible and left no spatial audit trail.

There was also no baseline surface available to compute volumes against — meaning the first step was establishing a reliable pre-stockpile reference surface from which to measure accumulation.

02
Approach

A reference DTM representing the pre-stockpile ground surface was generated from LiDAR ground returns and used as the base surface for all volume calculations. Stockpile boundary polygons were digitized from imagery and LiDAR intensity data to define the calculation extents precisely.

For LiDAR-based volumes, the DSM within each stockpile boundary was differenced against the reference DTM. The resulting cut-fill volume was integrated using raster cell area — producing volumetric estimates in cubic metres with full spatial traceability.

For drone SfM surveys, point clouds generated from photogrammetric processing were co-registered to the LiDAR coordinate system, then processed through the same differencing workflow — ensuring consistency between the two data sources and enabling change detection between survey periods.

Repeatable across survey periods

The workflow replaced manual tape surveys with a spatially explicit, reproducible process that produces volume estimates traceable to specific elevation surfaces and boundary polygons. Results are directly usable for compliance reporting without additional reconciliation.

Using both LiDAR and drone data within the same workflow also provided a practical model for tiered monitoring — LiDAR for periodic large-area snapshots, drone surveys for frequent targeted updates between LiDAR acquisitions.

Dual-source advantage Co-registering drone SfM and LiDAR to the same coordinate reference meant change detection between any two survey periods was consistent regardless of which platform collected the data — a significant improvement for long-term inventory tracking.
